Voter suppression


A Brief History


1700s: Voting Rights were solely given to White, Male landowners.

1800s: The passing of the 15th Amendment ensured that people could not be denied the right to vote based on race; half a million black men were now eligible to vote. However, the Southern States placed polling taxes and literacy tests to discourage Black men from excersing their right to vote. In 1869, Jim Crow Laws were established, enforcing segregation in the South.

1920: Women were given the right to vote with the ratification of the 19th amendment. Black Women, however, still faced widespread discrimination-especially in the South.

1965: The Voting Rights Act passes Congress, helping tear down barriers that limited African Americans and other marginalized groups from voting.

1972: Voting Rights Act is expanded to protect members of language minority groups; Required Voting Materials to be transcribed in different languages and provide multilingual aid.

2000: The Bush-Gore Presidential race led to a recount of votes in Florida, which ignited conversation over the issues regarding the U.S. election system. In response, President Bush passed the "Help America Vote Act" in 2002.

2019: Trump's attempt to add a citizenship question in the 2020 census was widely controversial. Advocacy groups claimed that this result would undercount the immigrant population and decrease their political power

Voter Suppression by the Numbers

In 2011, Kansas Secretary of State Kris Kobach championed a law requiring Kansans to show “proof of citizenship” documents in order to register to vote. Most people don’t carry the required documents on hand — like a passport, or a birth certificate — and as a result, the law blocked over 30,000 Kansans from voting.

In 2016, Arkansas purged thousands of voters for so-called felony convictions, even though some of the voters had never been convicted of a felony at all

In 2013, Virginia purged 39,000 voters based on data that was later found to have an error rate of up to 17 percent.
